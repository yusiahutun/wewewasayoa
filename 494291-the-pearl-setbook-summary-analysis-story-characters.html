    The Pearl Setbook, published in 1947, is one of the masterpieces written by the refined author John Steinbeck. The novel explores the deviant behaviour of man against some of the social conventions deemed acceptable. The story perfectly portrays greed in human beings and its consequences.

    The story in The Pearl set book revolves around a Mexican-Indian fisherman. While on his daily fishing errands, he finds the biggest pearl and wants to sell it in the capital city. In addition, the fisherman wants to acquire a huge sum of money to pay his debts and become one of the wealthiest people in his town, La Paz.

    It details the life and struggles of Kino, his wife, Juana, and their infant son, Coyotito. What is the setting of the book The Pearl? The Pearl is a story set in a small Mexican town called La Paz. The family of three lives humbly and peacefully in a hut by the shore where Kino does diving and angling to sustain and provide for his family.

    The Pearl Setbook notes and themes

    Novels and stories are written to shed light on various important ideas. This is no different for The Pearl Setbook, as it highlights several ideas. The Pearl summary and analysis bring out some of the following key themes:

    1. Family

    The book's major theme is family and the desire of every man to preserve and sustain it. Everything Kino does is in a bid to preserve his family. For instance, he wants to get the best medicament for his son. Second, after finding the pearl, giving his family a good life is all he can think of. He even kills some of the people who get in his way.

    2. Paradox

    When Kino finds the pearl, he believes he will be the wealthiest man in his town. But, on the contrary, it comes with lots of negativity. Instead of bringing good luck and solving all his problems, it causes destruction and creates more trouble.

    3. Wealth

    The novel brings out the theme of wealth as a bad omen. When Kino doesn't have wealth, he leads a peaceful and happy life with his family. However, immediately after he finds the pearl, hell starts breaking loose. He is nearly killed, forced to leave his home, and eventually, his son dies. However, when he returns the pearl to the ocean, he finds peace again.

    4. Greed

    Human greed is explicitly seen in several instances throughout the novel. When Kino finds the pearl, he refuses to sell it for a thousand pesos because he believes it is worth at least fifty times more. He is greedy to have more money despite several instances showing that the pearl only causes bad luck. His greed leads to the death of his only son.

    5. Gender

    The theme of gender in the Mexican coastal region is prevalent in the novel. The entire book has a few women, Juana (Kino's wife) and Apolonia (Juan's wife), while all the other characters are men. While men dominate the novel, the two women are secondary characters with little say.

    6. Power

    The novel sheds light on the power that comes with having money. When Kino finds the pearl, he knows he will get money, thus becoming more powerful. Also, many people, including the doctor, attempt to steal the pearl because they want a share of the power. He refuses to throw away the pearl because he fears losing power.

    7. Man and nature

    The Pearl brings out the theme of man and nature, showcasing that man will never win against nature. Immediately Kino finds the pearl; this marks the beginning of his fight with nature. However, he fails to conquer despite his persistence and stubbornness.
