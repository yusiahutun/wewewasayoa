As a result, many children of the '50s grew up with fond memories of wearing burlap sacks as costumes.

People With The Mummies Of Venzone, Italy, 1950s

A picturesque village nestled in the foothills of the Alps, it was an idyllic time for its inhabitants who enjoyed a peaceful and prosperous life. These ancient remains had been preserved by the dry mountain air and were a source of fascination for locals and visitors alike. It was even said that Alfred Hitchcock used them as inspiration when he filmed his classic thriller "Psycho" in 1960. A Micky Mouse Club Meeting, 1930s

(wikimedia commons)

Before the iconic Mickey Mouse Club television series, there was a theater-based Mickey Mouse Club that brought together young fans of Mickey Mouse. The first meeting of this original club took place on January 11, 1930, at the Fox Dome Theater in Ocean Park, California. The concept quickly gained popularity, and by March 31st of the same year, there were already 60 theaters hosting their own Mickey Mouse Clubs.

The club released its inaugural issue of the "Official Bulletin of the Mickey Mouse Club" on April 15, 1930, providing members with exclusive content and updates on their favorite characters. The club's popularity continued to soar, reaching an impressive one million members by 1932. The success of the club extended beyond the United States, and in 1933, the first British club opened in Darlington's Arcade Cinema.

However, as the 1930s progressed and with numerous clubs established worldwide, Disney began to phase out the theater-based Mickey Mouse Club. The focus shifted to other ventures, and the club's activities gradually diminished. Nevertheless, the early Mickey Mouse Club remains a testament to the enduring popularity and impact of Mickey Mouse and the imaginative world of Disney, laying the foundation for the beloved television series that would follow in later years.

Swiss/German bear-hunting suit from the 18th century

(wikimedia commons)

The Swiss/German bear-hunting suit from the 18th century, depicted in a picture by Malcolm Kirk, represents a unique and specialized form of protective clothing used during bear-hunting expeditions. This suit was crafted with the intent to provide the wearer with enhanced protection against bear attacks.

The suit was primarily made of leather, offering durability and flexibility. It featured wooden spikes strategically placed on various parts of the suit, such as the sleeves, shoulders, and legs. These spikes acted as a defensive measure, aiming to deter or injure the attacking bear.

To further reinforce the defensive capabilities of the suit, iron chains were incorporated into the design. The chains would serve as an additional layer of protection, acting as a barrier between the wearer and the powerful jaws or claws of the bear.

The bear-hunting suit showcases the ingenuity and resourcefulness of hunters in the 18th century, who sought ways to minimize the risks associated with such dangerous encounters. The suit's distinctive appearance and design reflect the unique challenges of bear hunting and the measures taken to ensure the safety of the hunter.

Malcolm Kirk's picture offers a glimpse into the historical context and aesthetics of bear-hunting attire, highlighting the innovative approach taken by hunters to safeguard themselves in the pursuit of their challenging and perilous task.

A Nine-Year-Old Girl Working, 1911 

(library of congress)

The photograph captures a poignant moment in the life of young Anna J. Gallant, known as Nan de Gallant, in Eastport, Maine, in 1911. Taken by Lewis Hine, the image sheds light on the challenging circumstances faced by working-class families during that era.

Nan, at just nine years old, can be seen working as a cartoner at the Seacoast Canning Co., Factory #2. Her mother and two sisters also work in the factory, with one sister earning an impressive $7 in a single day. The entire family, originally from Perry, Maine, migrates to Eastport for the summer months to engage in seasonal work.

The photograph reveals the harsh realities of labor during that time. The women in the family start their work as early as 7 a.m. and sometimes continue until midnight, demonstrating the demanding and exhausting nature of their jobs. Nan's brother, meanwhile, works on boats, highlighting the diverse employment opportunities available to different family members.

The photograph serves as a historical document, offering a glimpse into the lives of working-class families and the hardships they endured in early 20th-century America. It provides a powerful visual reminder of the social and economic conditions that prevailed during that time, prompting reflection on the progress made since then in terms of labor rights and child welfare.

Rat-proof trench bed for French soldiers in WW1

(reddit)

During WW1, French soldiers were equipped with a revolutionary new way to rest: the rat-proof trench bed. This innovative design was made of thick wooden boards and metal rods that could be quickly assembled in trenches for an impromptu night's sleep. It also featured a raised platform which allowed air to circulate beneath it, making it more comfortable than sleeping on the cold ground. The beds proved so successful that they were adopted by other military forces during the war, including the British army who dubbed them "trench hammocks". To this day, these ingenious designs remain a testament to the resourcefulness of those brave soldiers fighting in one of history's most devastating wars.

Spirit Photography (1922)

(Getty Images)

Spirit photography emerged as a fascinating and controversial practice in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. This form of photography claimed to capture images of spirits, ghosts, or supernatural entities alongside living subjects. Practitioners believed that through special techniques or mediums, they could unveil a realm beyond the visible world.

While some individuals embraced spirit photography as proof of an afterlife or communication with the spirit realm, others viewed it with skepticism, attributing the images to trickery, double exposures, or other photographic manipulations. The practice often involved using long exposure times, double exposures, or superimposing preexisting images onto new photographs. This created ethereal and sometimes eerie effects, giving the impression of otherworldly beings or phenomena coexisting with the living subjects.

Spirit photography sparked intense debates among believers and skeptics, with proponents passionately defending the authenticity of the images and their spiritual significance. Meanwhile, critics sought to debunk the phenomenon and expose any potential fraudulent practices.

While the popularity of spirit photography eventually waned as scientific skepticism grew, it remains an intriguing chapter in the history of photography and the exploration of the unknown. The photographs from this era continue to spark curiosity and serve as a reminder of humanity's enduring fascination with the mysterious and the supernatural.

A Drunken Man In Top Hat And Tails Clings To A Lamp-Post, London, 1934. 

(reddit)

London has been plagued by the notorious "London fog" phenomenon for centuries, earning the nickname "pea-soupers" due to their thick, yellow appearance. The city's struggles with polluted fog trace back to the 13th century when coal burning contributed to the issue. In the 1600s, complaints about smoke and pollution led to the implementation of legislation by King James I, although it proved ineffective. As industrialization surged in the late 1700s, London's situation worsened, with water vapor combining with coal particulates to create dense clouds known as smog, a term coined in the early 20th century. The Industrial Revolution and the rapid growth of the metropolis in the 19th century exacerbated air pollution, resulting in the fog-laden atmosphere depicted vividly in the works of Charles Dickens and Arthur Conan Doyle.

Some Paper Mache Heads From The First World War

(getty images)

During World War I, soldiers devised a creative and resourceful tactic to locate the position of an enemy sniper. They utilized paper mache heads as decoys to draw out the sniper's fire. These lightweight and easily maneuverable replicas were strategically placed in exposed positions, mimicking the appearance of soldiers on the battlefield. When the sniper fired at these decoys, the soldiers would quickly identify the direction of the shot, enabling them to pinpoint the enemy's hidden location. This ingenious use of paper mache heads showcased the ingenuity and adaptability of soldiers in the face of danger, as they employed unconventional methods to gain a tactical advantage on the battlefield.

Photograph Of A Victorian Baby Held By Their "Hidden Mother"

(public domain)

During the Victorian era, photography studios often employed creative techniques to accommodate the long exposure times or create captivating illusions. While spirit photography is well-known, there were other intriguing tricks utilized during that time.

One fascinating practice involved parents masquerading as a chair beneath a cloak, as documented in Linda Fregni Nagler's book, The Hidden Mother. Due to the lengthy exposure times required, young children found it challenging to remain still for their portraits. To overcome this, parents would hide themselves and hold their children in place. However, the resulting images had an eerie quality, with the hidden parents appearing like specters lurking behind their uneasy children. This added a sense of mystery and even a hint of mortality, especially for children, amplifying the ghostly presence and creating an unsettling atmosphere.

These photographs with concealed parents not only provide a glimpse into the technical challenges of early photography but also reflect the prevailing cultural beliefs and anxieties of the time. The Victorian era was marked by a heightened awareness of mortality, with high infant and child mortality rates. Consequently, the ghostly appearances in these portraits took on a more ominous and poignant significance.

‘Hells Cafe’ In Paris 1920s

(Getty Images)

Cabaret de L'Enfer, a renowned establishment in the vibrant neighborhood of Montmartre, holds a captivating place in history. Founded in November 1892 by Antonin Alexander, this unique cabaret invited patrons into a world of dark enchantment. Its name, which translates to "Cabaret of Hell," set the stage for an unforgettable experience. With its macabre decor, adorned with devilish imagery, and its talented performers, Cabaret de L'Enfer aimed to transport its audience to a realm of fiery fantasies. However, in 1950, the captivating legacy of this cabaret came to an end when it was demolished to make way for the expansion of a Monoprix supermarket. While only remnants of its existence remain, Cabaret de L'Enfer remains a testament to the avant-garde spirit and vibrant artistic culture that once flourished in Montmartre.
