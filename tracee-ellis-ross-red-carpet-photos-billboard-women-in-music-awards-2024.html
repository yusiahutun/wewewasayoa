    Tracee Ellis Ross Is the Blueprint for Having Fun on the Red Carpet

    Does anyone ever have more fun getting dressed up than Tracee Ellis Ross? My instinctive answer is a firm no. The Black-ish alum frequently makes a spectacle out of red-carpet fashion, pushing and playing with the boundaries of what it means for an A-lister to adhere to a black-tie dress code.

    01 Jan 0001, 00:00

    Does anyone ever have more fun getting dressed up than Tracee Ellis Ross? My instinctive answer is a firm no. The Black-ish alum frequently makes a spectacle out of red-carpet fashion, pushing and playing with the boundaries of what it means for an A-lister to adhere to a black-tie dress code.

    Just take her latest red-carpet appearance as an example. Last night, Ross hosted the 2024 Billboard Women in Music Awards ceremony wearing an outfit that took the red-carpet cutout trend to new heights. Her colorblock midi dress by Loewe featured various swaths of fabric in black, gray, red, and mint being draped across her body, leaving gaping cutouts on the sides of her torso and at the front of her abdomen.

    Michael Tran//Getty Images

    The unique piece deserved equally unique accessories, which Ross delivered tenfold. These included black leather round toe heels with a dramatic trumpet heel, a pair of two large gemstone rings in green and orange, a stack of gold chain bracelets punctuated by a large orange oval-shaped crystal, and black bead dangle earrings.

    Gilbert Flores//Getty Images

    Ross’s experimental sense of style has been on full display over the past few months, from the actor attending a screening in Loewe’s viral crochet sweater to her stepping out in New York City dressed in two extremely puffy jackets.

    She previously opened up about finding her own distinct sense of style in a 2022 interview with Elle Canada.

    “Dressing up has always been how I express my creativity,” she told the magazine. “For a while, especially in high school and during my teenage years, clothing was like my armour, a way to protect myself. If my outfit was good, I felt that I could conquer the entire world. Later, in my 20s and 30s, when I began to feel more comfortable in my own skin, fashion became one of the most important means of my self-expression. I loved fashion and dressing well. Moreover, clothing—whether it’s a T-shirt with some text on it or an incredible suit—can be a statement about who you are.”
