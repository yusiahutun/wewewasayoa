    Ile de R is just so kick-ass: Donkeys are a pure delight in French haven

    I was hoping to see Mimi in her special blue outfit – a pin-striped number, a bit like a trouser suit.

    Admittedly, it’s a Friday, so there’s no reason for her to have donned her Sunday best.

    So I bite my tongue and greet her graciously – with a pat on her fat white nose and her thick, straggly back.

    Mimi is one of Ile de Ré’s ânes en culotte, or donkeys in culottes – the pantaloons or breeches which are the animals’ trademark attire.

    The clothes have turned Mimi and her mates into celebrities, with weekly photoshoots on the green near St Martin lighthouse.

    Their outfits date back to the 19th century, when their predecessors (members of a rare, scruffy breed the Baudet du Poitou) used to transport Ile de Ré’s salt.

    The donkeys got so badly bitten by mosquitoes in the salt marshes that peasants made them breeches to protect their legs.

    Today, everyone speaks of the island’s “donkeys in pyjamas”.

    “But Mimi’s culottes really are pyjamas,” claims Yvette, the donkey’s 80-year-old owner.

    “I made them myself, from a pair my husband wore in bed.”

    I give Mimi a heartfelt stroke – no one should have to wear PJs in public.

    My guide Sylvain, whose dreadlocks almost match Mimi’s (but at least you can see his eyes), suggests it’s time we move on.

    We’re exploring Ile de Ré by bike, which is what most people do when they visit. Cycling round the island is easy since it’s small (12 miles long and three miles wide), flat and criss-crossed with paths.

    It’s also easy to get to, as it’s just nine miles from La Rochelle airport and linked to the mainland by a bridge. Unsurprisingly, perhaps, accessible island charm comes at a cost – restaurants charge London prices and hotels are expensive.

    But I discover a great alternative, a five-star campsite where I don’t even need a tent.

    The Sunêlia Interlude, run by Sunêlia Vacances at Le Bois Plage en Ré on the south coast, offers the option of deluxe chalets.

    Mine, set among pines, has lots of glass windows (just watch where you change), an en-suite and a kitchen with dishwasher, oven and fridge.

    Not that I plan to do any cooking. I’ll be dining at Interlude’s restaurant – well, wouldn’t you, if the menu includes oysters and chips?

    Down from the restaurant is the campsite’s bike hire shop where Sylvain, the entertainment manager, offers cycle tours for free.

    Mine lasts several hours and includes a fortuitous meeting with Mimi and Yvette, the town of Le Bois Plage en Ré and villages of whitewashed houses with shuttered windows.

    “We can paint the shutters whatever colour we want,” Sylvain jokes, “as long as it’s blue or green.”

    On the north coast we stop at St Martin de Ré, whose star-shaped citadel was built by Vauban, one of France’s great engineers.

    Sylvain suggests an ice cream and takes me to La Martinière on the quay. Flavours include oyster, salted caramel and island potato.

    One tray is labelled “schtroumph”. “Smurf?” I ask, bewildered.

    It’s very blue, so Sylvain nods. It tastes remarkably similar to marzipan. Back at the campsite’s beach I try the latest version of supping – stand-up paddle boarding.

    The board, which I share with five others, is as big as a bed.

    We greet the waves with gusto and stay afloat for all of five minutes. The next day I visit Phare des Baleines, a 19th century lighthouse on the isle’s western tip and the harbour town of La Flotte where bijou shops sell seafood, salt and soap.

    The soap is from milk provided by Mimi and her friends.

    “It leaves your skin as soft as Cleopatra’s,” Sylvain says.

    “Mimi’s a clever girl.” I can’t deny the donkey’s got it made. She may have to parade in pyjamas but her fame is island-wide.
